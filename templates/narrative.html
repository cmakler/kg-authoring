Scarcity and Choice

At its core, economics is about the choices we face -- as a individuals, or as part of a firm, or as a society. We have at our disposal various resources (time, money) and various ways to spend those resources.

The budget constraint partitions the available choice space into affordable and unaffordable choices. The standard budget constraint for $n$ goods may be written as $$p_1x_1 + p_2x_2 + \cdots + p_nx_n \le m$$ where $p_i$ is the price of good $i$, and $x_i$ is the quantity of good $i$. More generally, using vector notation, we can write this constraint as a dot product: $$\vec{p} \cdot \vec{x} \le m$$ where $\vec{p} = [p_1,p_2,...p_n]$ is a vector of the prices of $n$ goods, and $\vec{x} = [x_1,x_2,...,x_n]$ is a vector of quantities of $n$ goods.

Note that each of the terms in this constraint is measured in dollars: for each good $i$, the term $p_ix_i$ represents the expenditure on good $i$:$$\overbrace{\underbrace{p_i}_\frac{\text{dollars}}{\text{units of i}} \times \underbrace{x_i}_\text{units of i}}^\text{dollars}$$

The budget line for two goods

Visually, it's useful to constrain ourselves to two goods, since that's easy to plot. In Good 1 - Good 2 space, the "budget line" $p_1x_1 + p_2x_2 = m$ partitions Good 1 - Good 2 space into the set of affordable bundles (beneath the budget line) and the set of unaffordable bundles (above the budget line). All bundles \textit{along} the budget line cost the same amount, and exhaust the available income $m$.
